Exploring The Uncharted: Navigating Off-The-Beaten-Path Terrain For Solo Budget Backpackers

Michael Boroda
December 11, 2023

“Man cannot discover new oceans unless he has the courage to lose sight of the shore.” – Andre Gide

Yes, sometimes, in order to uncover the world’s most beautiful destinations, we’re required to blaze our own trails. I’ll be 100% honest with you, gentlemen; I have not yet backpacked to unfrequented destinations.

The reason that I write this article is because I see backpackers asking questions about them all the time, and I thought I’d help out. Furthermore, I know that Europe gets crowded with tourists, and when that happens, we want seclusion.

That said, privacy doesn’t have to mean locking yourself in a hostel room, or going home. It can also mean travelling to quieter or more remote destinations that offer a reserved experience compared to the touristy places of the world. Many backpackers think that travelling is exclusively about visiting hotspots, and it doesn’t have to be. In fact, wayfaring to the backwoods – or quiet locales – can be just as rewarding (if not more so) as experiencing popular sites.

Not only do less-trodden trails hone your resilience – since they challenge you mentally, physically, and spiritually –; they can also be inexpensive (not to mention fun!). Plus, you’ll have stories to share with your friends, family, and colleagues when you get back.

So without further ado, here are 6 off-the-beaten-path destinations to visit in Europe:

TABLE OF CONTENTS:

1.     Faroe Islands, Denmark

2.     Maramures, Romania

3.     Gimmelwald, Switzerland

4.     Tusheti, Georgia

5.     Orkney Islands, Scotland

6.     Svalbard, Norway

1.     Faroe Islands, Denmark

The Faroes are a group of islets located on the North Atlantic Ocean. Though they sit 320 KM (approx. 200 miles) Northwest of the U.K., they belong to the Danish Realm.

Why do I recommend backpacking to these islands? Well, gentlemen, because they offer rugged landscapes, dramatic cliffs, and picturesque fjords in addition to authenticity.

 

Furthermore, these islands are perfect for seclusion – as their untraveled terrain means less crowds and tourism.

If you’re someone who likes to travel to lesser-known destinations, this is a great place to stop over.

Make sure you plan ahead, though, as the inaccessibility of this archipelago makes it difficult to reach, and dangerous to trek. Luckily there are plenty of hostels and Airbnbs, so you shouldn’t have trouble finding accommodation.

I want to emphasize the importance of packing light and smart for places like this as every extra pound can become a burden. Make sure to cram only the essentials into your bag, and DEFINITELY have your navigation apps handy.

2.     Maramures, Romania

Considering the war in Ukraine, it might be a good idea to avoid this area for now. Nonetheless, I think it’s worth mentioning how cool of a place to visit this would be during normal, untroubled times. I still think it’s a beneficial to know about this region, which is located in Northern Romania, and borders Western Ukraine.

Maramures is situated in the Northeastern Carpathian Mountains, near the Tisza River. Known for its timber churches, gates, and longstanding traditions, this is a great place to visit if you want to get an authentic peak at Romanian village life – which has remained unchanged for centuries.

There’re tons of scenic landscapes, including rolling hills, forests, and streams. Likewise, because of the area’s limited tourism infrastructure, it offers a serene and unspoiled natural environment free of crowds. Don’t worry, though, there’re still accommodations and tourist services (just not as much as at more popular destinations).

In addition to enduring fewer crowds, Maramures is known for its Artisan traditions, including woodworking, pottery, and textiles. Backpackers can witness craftsmen at work, as well as participate in unique festivals that celebrate local customs.

Meanwhile, the region’s inhabitants are known for their warm hospitality, so rest assured that they’d make you feel welcome, and provide you with an authentic experience.

When visiting this place, make sure to carry a first-aid kit, emergency shelter, and a satellite phone as you never know what sorts of weather conditions might pop up, or if you’ll get lost.

Also make a point of fostering connections with locals, as learning from and respecting their cultures can enhance your safety and provide you with insights. 

3.     Gimmelwald, Switzerland

This small, traffic-free community in the Bernese Oberland stands at an elevation of 1363 meters (or 4472 feet). It’s located beneath the Jungfrau-Aletsch Protected Area – a UNESCO World Heritage site – and is also part of the inventory of Swiss heritage. 

Gimmelwald is so remote that access by car is impossible due to a lack of road connection. If you want to get up here, you’ll have to hike, or take a cable car.

If this sounds off-the-beaten-path enough for you, then this is one heck of a place to check out. Not only is the village tourist and traffic-free, but it’s also a great base for hiking and appreciating nature - also the alpine smell is to die for.

In addition to the parish’s seclusion, it offers breathtaking views of the mountains, waterfalls, and trails, so if you’re into photography or exercise, this is your jam. The hamlet is quiet due to its altitude, and it’s an ideal place for peace, relaxation, and meditation.

For all you hiking enthusiasts, this neighborhood offers trails to Schilthorn, and Müren, two of Switzerland’s most iconic spots. On behalf of those who are interested in a rustic experience, Gimmelwald is a place where travellers can appreciate the authentic Swiss mountain village lifestyle, with chalets and a friendly, close-knit community.

Still, because of the limited commercialization of the spot, the tourist amenities are restricted. You can find hostels, guesthouses, and Airbnbs, just make sure to book in advance, as spaces are limited.

Finally, make sure to minimize your environmental impact by practicing Leave No Trace principles, such as:

·      Planning ahead and preparing (i.e. knowing regulations, weather conditions, repackaging food)

·      Traveling and camping on durable surfaces (i.e. maintained trails, rock, gravel, sand, dry grasses, or snow, camping 200ft away from lakes and rivers)

·      Disposing of waste properly (i.e. packing out toiletry & hygiene products, garbage, compost, and litter)

·      Leaving what you find (i.e. not touching or moving rocks, plants, or other natural objects, avoiding transporting or introducing non-native species)

·      Minimizing campfire impacts (i.e. using a lightweight stove and candle lanterns, putting out fires)

·      Respecting wildlife (i.e. respecting biota from a distance; not following or approaching)

·      Being considerate of other visitors (i.e. avoiding making loud noises, practicing trail courtesy)

And now onto…

4.     Tusheti, Georgia

Located in the Caucasus Mountains, Tusheti is a remote and high-altitude region known for its stunning landscapes, ancient villages, and unique culture. It's accessible only during the summer months because the road leading to the region is blocked off by snow and ice before early June and after late September.

Also, gentlemen, I should warn you that the motorway leading to the area is infamously dangerous, so I wouldn’t recommend navigating the roads yourself. Instead, your best bet is to hire a driver who knows the road and has experience navigating it. Never… EVER… drive with someone you don’t know or trust, and don’t even get me started on alcohol or hitchhiking – both of which are major no-no’s on a highway lined with the tombstones of drivers who lost their lives on the Abano Pass (the route leading to and from Tusheti).

All this to say: you need to be EXTREMELY careful when travelling to this village. You also need to be vigilant while lodging, since pickpockets abound (although that’s anywhere in Europe).

Otherwise, if you love remote locations, then Tusheti is an ideal stop on your journey because it’s located far from urban centres and tourist hubs. Furthermore, as I mentioned, it’s accessed through challenging, winding, and steep roads that are impassable during the winter months.

Once you get there, though, you’re in for a treat as the district offers scenic beauty – natural landscapes, alpine meadows, rugged peaks, and pristine rivers –, making it a paradise for backpackers, hikers, and outdoor lovers.  There’re tons of hiking and trekking opportunities, including routes that traverse mountain passes and connect remote settlements.

The region also offers tons of cultural heritages that include unique traditions, architecture, and festivals. If you’re into old-fashioned customs, Tusheti is sprinkled with mountain villages that rely on husbandry, since wildlife such as eagles, bears, and ibex dwell in the area. If you’re into wildlife and bird watching, this is the place for you.

While Tusheti lacks the commercialization and tourist infrastructure of popular destinations, it provides an authentic and unspoiled experience. The isolation of this region means that it’s relatively untouched by mass tourism, preserving its natural and cultural heritage.

Visiting Tusheti requires careful planning, though, as well as a sense of adventure, and an appreciation for remote and rugged topography. If you possess all that, you should be good to go – travellers who seek to explore a pristine and less-visited part of the world will find Tusheti to be a truly off-the-beaten-path destination. Just make sure to source safe drinking water and plan your meals in advance, since access to provisions might be limited.

5.     Orkney Islands, Scotland

While not undiscovered, the Orkney Islands offer a quieter, and more nature-focused travel experience compared to major European hotspots. Backpackers who appreciate historical sites, natural beauty, and a sense of tranquillity will find this archipelago to be an appealing, off-the-beaten-path destination.

The islands are located on the North Sea, far from land, and their remote location contributes to a sense of isolation and adventure. Situated on the Northeastern coast of Scotland, Orkney covers prehistoric sites, sandstone cliffs, and seal colonies.

As mentioned, Orkney is known for its archaeological spots – like the Ring of Brodgar –, which aren’t as renowned as other landmarks. Moreover, the islands’ natural beauties – including landscapes, coastlines, hills, and beaches – make them a perfect spot for hiking, and wildlife observation.

Influenced by Norse and Scottish traditions, Orkney has a rich history and culture that travellers can explore through museums and local customs. Mainland, the largest of the islands, includes Skara Brae – a conserved village – and Maeshowe – a mausoleum with 12th century Viking carvings.

The isles also have a nautical legacy that includes boatbuilding and fishing; these practises make up the economy and culture.

Meanwhile, parts of this destination are delegated as Dark Sky Preserves – areas committed to eliminating light pollution –, making them ideal spots for stargazing.

While The Orkney Islands receive visitors – particularly during peak season –, they’re not as congested as major tourist spots, allowing for a peaceful experience.

6.     Svalbard, Norway

Last but not least on our list is one of the world’s northernmost inhabited areas: a Norwegian archipelago between Norway and the North Pole.

Positioned in the Arctic Ocean, Svalbard is a truly off-the-beaten-path destination. It offers rocky, remote terrain that includes glaciers, fjords, and tundra. The wildlife consists of polar bears, reindeer, seabirds, and white foxes, so if you’re into photography, you won’t be bored.

When visiting during the summer months, you’ll witness 24-hour sunlight – AKA the “midnight sun”. Winter stopovers will offer The Northern Lights.

Since this landmass is located closer to the North Pole than Europe, its population is small, with residents living in the administrative centre of Longyearbyen. The limited populace provides a sense of isolation and wilderness – perfect for off-the-beaten-path travel.

Be warned, though: the harsh climate – frigid temperatures, long nights, and polar daylights – makes it challenging to backpack, so plan well in advance.

Otherwise, there’s tons of adventure in Svalbard – including polar bear expeditions, ice caving, snowmobiling, and dogsledding – as well as untouched, natural beauty.

Now, getting to Svalbard requires flying in from mainland Norway, typically from Oslo or Tromsø. While the limited flights and remote location make it less accessible for mass tourism, you’ll have no trouble getting there, as you’re an off-the-beaten-path traveller. Make sure to prepare for the severe climate, wildlife encounters, and rough terrain that make Svalbard an unforgettable destination.

As you can see, guys, there’re plenty of uncharted, and budget-friendly places to visit in Europe. When prepping for your trip, make sure to plan ahead and do your research on the culture, customs, landscapes, weather conditions, wildlife, and potential risks of the destination you’re scheduling to visit. Remember to purchase travel insurance! I cannot stress this enough. Never go on vacation without the proper coverage.
